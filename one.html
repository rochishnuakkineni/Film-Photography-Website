Film photography is the 2nd in 3 eras of photography technology so far (PetaPixel). It’s after the original plate era (where the first photograph by Louis Daguerre was taken on January 7, 1893) and before the still growing digital era of photography today (started in the mid 1970s by Kodak scientists) (PetaPixel).


1884

The Film era started in Rochester, New York, 1884 by dry plate manufacturer George Eastman and camera inventor William Hall Walker, who together invented the tools and procedures for a photographic film coated onto malleable material (PetaPixel and PBS). Eastman sold the film roll adapter intended for cameras at the time but struggled to make sales because of the separate equipment needed to shoot the rolls and how the film itself was "harder to process than the glass plate photography technology at the time" (PetaPixel).

1888

To combat this, the pair made the “Kodak”, the first film roll camera and preloaded it with enough film for 100 photos (PetaPixel). Customers who purchased the camera would then mail it back to Eastman’s Kodak company to have their cameras reloaded, photos processed and printed (PetaPixel). The reloaded camera and prints would then be mailed back to customers. The camera costed $25 (~$750 today) while developing, printing and reloading was around $10 (~$300 today) (PetaPixel).





1899


Edward Raymond Turner patented plans for a color motion film.





1900

the lower price end Brownie Kodack camera was introduced.



The start of the 20th century ushered in the Film Era of photography with the Eastman Kodak company holding the majority of film technology market share at the time. Later in the decade, more advancements in this technology would be accomplished by companies Nikon, Canon, Pentax, etc.

1906

In 1906, Kinemacolor run by George Albert Smith released the first fully functional color film system (Baldegg, 2012). It worked by shooting photo frames on red and green filters and projecting each layer on each other (Baldegg, 2012).




Interesting Fact, the oldest color film reel that's been found recently dates back to 1902 and was shot by Edward Raymond Turner (Vartanian, 2012). Kinemacolor's process had its roots paved by Turner who died of a heartattack in 1903 (Vartanian, 2012).



1932

More experiments to produce functioning forms of color film were further in production by companies like Technicolor in 1914 (Deguzman, 2021). However In 1932, after years of work they were successful in establishing color film techniques from their expensive dye transferring process using 3 color film strips of green, red and blue. Some famous movies using the basis of these techniques include 1939’s The Wizard of Oz and Gone With the Wind (Deguzman, 2021).

1936

Kodachrome color film was released. It also came with standardized 35 mm film cassettes allowing any film to be used on all 35mm cameras (PetaPixel).

1963

Kodak’s Instamatic series started with the Instamatic 50. These line of cameras were designed to fit in people’s pockets all the while being even cheaper and easier to use. It was also equipped with 110-126 film roll cartridges for shooting (Science Museum Group).

1970

At EXPO ‘70 in Fuji Pavilion, Osaka, Japan, the IMAX experience was introduced via their camera, projector and domed screen system producing a cinematic experience running on film technology (Brown).
